Covid-19 Gym Relief Overview

Federal, state, and local governments have provided a variety of assistance programs. Every little bit helps. We’ve collected a list of resources and grant programs here that will help us all make it through these tough times.

Overview

Things are moving quickly and changing every day. Federal, state, and local governments have passed a variety of relief programs. We are tracking the ones that might affect gyms, including ours. The content below provides a summary of each program and how you can apply for the relief.

The following are the programs that we are aware of and are tracking:

  • Economic Injury Disaster Loan Program (EIDL)
  • EIDL Advance
  • Paycheck Protection Program
  • Refundable Employee Retention Credit
  • Emergency Family and Medical Leave Expansion Act

Things are changing quickly and this document will be updated as we receive new and better information. This document was last updated on April 2, 2020. This is not legal advice, we highly suggest you get the assistance of an accountant or a lawyer or both.

Economic Injury Disaster Loan Program (EIDL)

The SBA's economic injury disaster loan program has been expanded to cover small businesses that have been affected by COVID-19.

Key Facts

  • Loan value up to $2M
  • Capped at 3.75% interest
  • Repayment period on a case-by-case basis up to 30 years
  • Collateral required if loan value exceeds $25,000
  • All payments (principal and interest) deferred for 1 year
  • Can be used for debts, payroll, accounts payable and other bills
  • Apply now at https://covid19relief.sba.gov/#/

Do I Qualify?

  • The EIDL program applies to businesses with less than 500 employees, sole proprietors, independent contractors, and self-employed individuals. If you are a gym owner or personal instructor, you may be eligble.
  • You must be in a state that has been officially impacted by COVID-19. As of April 1, 2020, every state qualifies.
  • Your gym must have suffered “substantial economic injury” from COVID-19. This seems likely given that gyms have been often deemed non-essential businesses and forced to shut down.
  • There is some amount of qualification as applicants may be denied if they are deemed unable to repay.

What can I use the money for?

  • Existing debt payments
  • Owner salary and employee payroll
  • Utilities
  • Mortgage or rent

Conditions

  • 3.75% interest and up to 30 year term
  • May require collateral for loans over $25,000
  • No personal guarantees needed for loans up to $200,000
  • Loans over $200,000, require personal guarantees by entities owning more than 20% of the business
  • The EIDL loan is not forgiveable except for the $10,000 EIDL Advance. If you are not approved for an EIDL, the $10,000 advance turns into a grant that does not need to repaid. The $10,000 EIDL Advance does count against any PPP loan forgiveness.

Connections with other programs

  • You can apply for both an EIDL and the PPP, but the money cannot be used for the same purpose. E.g. the money from an EIDL and PPP can't both be used to pay rent. Also, money from PPP can be used to cover interest on debt, whereas EIDL money can be used to pay down both principal and interest. PPP loans are also forgiveable under certain circumstances. Given these differences, if you apply and receive both, some thought should be given to how money should be spent from each source of money.
  • An EIDL advance of up to $10,000 can be issued within three days after applying for an EIDL and need not be repaid if the EIDL application is denied.

EIDL Advance

We're highlighting this one in its own section because it is so important. As part of the EIDL application process it is possible to receive an initial advance of up to $10,000 within three days of application. If the application is denied, the amount turns into a grant and does not need to be repaid. Note that the $10,000 grant does count against any PPP loan forgiveness.

Paycheck Protection Program (PPP)

A key feature of the Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security Act (CARES Act) is the Paycheck Protection Program (PPP), a new loan program is also adminstered by the SBA.

PPP loans will be made by participating commercial lenders between now and June 30, 2020. BUT you should make sure to obtain your loan prior to May 5, 2020 in order to benefit from the full forgiveness period. This is becuase the covered period for loan uses ends June 30, 2020, and the forgiveness period is eight weeks.

There is still a good amount of uncertainty around this new loan program. The SBA has already declared that loans would carry a 0.5% interest rate before changing it shortly thereafter to 1% on Thursday April 2, 2020. As of the night before the program is set to launch, lenders who would be administering the loans have also indicated a lack of clarity and requested additional guidance from the federal government (see https://www.marketwatch.com/story/lenders-call-on-washington-for-more-clarity-on-small-business-rescue-program-a-day-before-launch-friday-2020-04-02?mod=mw_latestnews).

Key Facts

  • Forgivable loan valued at 2.5x monthly payroll (max $10M)
  • Portions of the loan that are not forgiven are capped at 4% interest with a 10 year maturity, although the SBA has indicated that the loans will be set at a 1% interest and 2 year maturity (last updated April 2, 2020)
  • No collateral or personal guarantees required
  • Can be used for payroll, employee benefits, commissions, interest related to existing debt and mortgages, rent, and utilities
  • Banks should be starting to process loan applications starting April 3, 2020. Learn more at https://www.sba.gov/funding-programs/loans/paycheck-protection-program-ppp
  • A sample form can be found at https://www.sba.gov/document/sba-form--paycheck-protection-program-ppp-sample-application-form

Do I Qualify?

  • The PPP program applies to businesses with less than 500 employees, sole proprietors, independent contractors, and self-employed individuals. If you are a gym owner or personal instructor, you may be eligble.
  • You must have been in business prior to February 15, 2020.
  • You must certify the following four things:
    1. The uncertainty of current economic conditions makes the loan request necessary to support the ongoing operations of the applicant.
    2. Funds will be used to retain workers and maintain payroll or make mortgage payments, lease payments or utility payments.
    3. You do not have an application pending for an SBA business loan for the same purpose and duplicative of amounts applied for or received under a covered loan.
    4. During the period beginning on February 15, 2020, and ending on December 31, 2020, you have not received another SBA business loan for the same purpose and duplicative of PPP amounts applied for or received.

How do I Apply?

  • A sample form can be found at https://www.sba.gov/document/sba-form--paycheck-protection-program-ppp-sample-application-form
  • You should probably have the following documents nearby:
    1. Tax returns
    2. Rent, lease, utility details for 2019 and 2020
    3. P&L
  • You can apply through any existing SBA 7(a) lender or through any federally insured depository institution, federally insured credit union, and Farm Credit System institution that is participating. Other regulated lenders will be available to make these loans once they are approved and enrolled in the program. You should consult with your local lender as to whether it is participating in the program.

What can I use the money for?

  • Payroll (forgiveable)
  • Healthcare benefits
  • Utilities (forgiveable)
  • Rent (forgiveable)
  • Interest on existing mortgages (forgivable)
  • Interest on existing debt

Conditions

  • Loan must be made before June 30, 2020.
  • No collateral and no personal guarantee required
  • Payments deferred for at least first 6 months and up to a year
  • Any portion of the loan that is not forgiven may be subject to an interest rate up to 4% with a 10 year maturity, although the SBA has inidicated that the loans will be set at a 1% interest and 2 year maturity (last updated April 2, 2020).

How do I calculate payroll costs?

  • Payroll costs include the total amount of any compensation to both:
    1. employees in the form of a salaries (not in excess of an annual salary of $100,000, prorated for the covered period), wages, commissions, cash tips, payments for leave, severance, group health care benefits, retirement benefits, or state or local taxes assessed on employee compensation; and
    2. a sole proprietor or independent contractor in the form of wages, commissions, income or similar payments not to exceed $100,000 per sole proprietor or independent contractor in one year prorated for the covered period.
  • Payroll costs do not include:
    1. Taxes imposed or withheld under chapters 21, 22, and 24 of the IRS code, compensation of employees whose principal place of residence is outside the US, and qualified sick or family leave for which credit is allowed under the Families First Coronavirus Response Act

What is this $100,000 limit?

For any employee with a salary at or more than $100,000, only $8,333 per month can be included in the calculation of average monthly payroll costs, in addition to that employee’s cost of health/retirement benefits and state and local taxes prorated for the covered period.

How do I calculate average payroll costs?

  • If you were in business February 15, 2019 – June 30, 2019: Your max loan is equal to 2.5X your average monthly payroll costs during that time period. If your business employs seasonal workers, you can opt to choose March 1, 2019 as your time period start date.
  • If you were not in business between February 15, 2019 – June 30, 2019: Your max loan is equal to 2.5X your average monthly payroll costs between January 1, 2020 and February 29, 2020.

How does Loan Forgiveness Work?

  • The federal government has indicated that because they expect a lot of people to get this loan, they expect that no more than 25% of the forgiven amount may be for non-payroll costs (see https://www.sba.gov/funding-programs/loans/paycheck-protection-program-ppp).
  • If you are given the $10,000 EIDL grant, the $10,000 is deducted from your forgiveness amount.
  • Loan amounts spent on documented payroll, mortgage interest, rent and utilities during the 8 week period after the loan is granted BUT only until June 30, 2020 can be forgiven (the "Forgivable Amount"). Amounts spent on other obligations will not be forgiven.
  • The Forgivable Amount will be reduced by both of the following:
    1. Multiplying the Forgiveable Amount by the average number of FTEs per month the recipient employed during February 15, 2020, and June 30, 2020 divided by the average number of FTEs per month that the company had either:
      1. From February 15, 2019, through June 30, 2019, or
      2. From January 1, 2020, through February 29, 2020.

      You may choose which denominator will result in a lower reduction. For the purposes of calculating FTEs, the average number of FTEs is determined by calculating the average number of FTEs for each pay period falling within a month.

    2. The amount of any reduction in the total salary or wages of any employee during the period February 15, 2020, and June 30, 2020 that was in excess of 25% of the employee’s salary or wages during the most recent full quarter prior to the period February 15, 2020, and June 30, 2020.

What if I fired some employees?

Where layoffs or salary reductions occurred between February 15, 2020, and April 26, 2020, the loan forgiveness reduction will not recognize those if the reductions in personnel or decrease in salaries are undone prior to June 30, 2020.

Do I have to hire back all the same people?

Things are not 100% clear at the moment, and everyone is awaiting additional guidance from the SBA. It seems what matters is average FTE over the relevant periods, so it doesn't seem like it has to be the same people.

The "Affiliations" Issue

There's been a lot of commentary written online about whether gyms will be affected by the "affiliations" issue with respect to calculating the number of employees. The SBA considers entities “affiliates” of one another when one has the power to control the other or a third party has the power to control both.

Although there are many paths to being an affiliate, the most common is through ownership. So that's what we will cover here. A majority owner constitutes an affiliate. A minority owner constitutes an affiliate if it can either prevent a quorom of the board or stockholders OR if it can veto day-to-day operational decisions of the company. In either case, if the owner controls other companies, the employees of those other companies will count towards your head count.
